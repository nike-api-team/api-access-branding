The Gathering

A global team descends on the desert

Over 25 professional trail runners from the United States, France, Japan, Korea, and beyond arrived in Palm Springs for the ACG Racing Department's winter performance camp. Rain and shine, they clocked some of their biggest volume of the year to date on the rugged desert terrain of the Coachella Valley — surrounded by scientists, designers, and engineers who came to listen, measure, and learn.

These athletes are unlike other runners. They don't just cover ground — they climb it. Specialists ranging from 20-minute vertical kilometer racers to 20-hour ultramarathon runners, the common thread is the mountain itself.

Camp Dispatch

Trail running is one step closer to human survival than most other endurance sports. The durations are ultra-long, the terrain is unforgiving, and the cognitive load is relentless — every footstrike on unstable ground is a real-time risk calculation.

Desert terrain, mountain air, and full commitment.
The Science

Four studies. One question: who is the trail athlete?

The API team set up a mobile performance lab at the Ritz Carlton Rancho Mirage and ran four concurrent research protocols throughout the week. The aim wasn't just to test — it was to build a comprehensive physiological profile of the trail runner, coupling who they are with what the sport demands.

Eyes over the mask. Every athlete tested.
Speed × Gradient
How does lactate threshold shift across different inclines? Athletes ran treadmill intervals at -5%, 0%, +5%, and +15% grade while blood lactate, muscle oxygen, and whole-body VO₂ were measured. The goal: build gradient-specific pacing maps.
Muscle Oxidative Capacity
Near-infrared sensors and blood flow occlusion cuffs measured how quickly the quads and calves consume and recover oxygen — before and after runs of varying duration. A window into muscle resilience and fatigue resistance.
Hemoglobin Mass
By tagging hemoglobin with trace carbon monoxide, the team mapped total blood volume, red blood cell volume, and oxygen-carrying capacity. These values reveal endurance potential and the effects of altitude and heat adaptation.
Cognitive Assessment
Through structured one-on-one conversations, a performance neuroscientist explored how each athlete approaches their race mentally — how cognitive decline takes shape during dark hours of pain and duration, and what it might take to recover. A keen interest in understanding just how deeply scrambled the brain gets.
The Speed × Gradient test in action — chasing threshold across inclines.
The Mind

Cognitive demands at the edge of fatigue

Trail running isn't just physical. At hour 15, fatigued and deep in the mountains, these athletes are still making split-second decisions — will that rock hold my weight? Can I cut this switchback without going over the edge? Should I push this climb and risk paying for it five hours later?

The camp addressed this head-on: morning mindfulness sessions with a performance neuroscientist and a clinical psychologist explored breathwork, sensory grounding, and visualization. Evening seminars around the fire covered topics from altitude adaptation to eccentric muscle loading to overnight fueling strategies.

Rather than wiring athletes with EEG headsets, the performance neuroscientist took a different approach — sitting with each athlete individually to explore how they experience cognitive decline during racing, what strategies they use to stay sharp at 3am, and how deeply scrambled their brain feels in the hours and days after a 50K or 100K. Understanding those patterns is a competitive frontier.

From shoes to vests to shirts to hats — everything must work together across hours of variable terrain, weather, and effort. The Signal

What we learned

Five days, four studies, dozens of conversations. The major takeaways from the desert:

01
Vertical changes everything
Lactate threshold, pacing strategy, muscle recruitment — the rules shift dramatically with gradient. Flat-ground assumptions don't hold on the mountain. Building gradient-specific metabolic profiles is essential for this athlete.
02
The system is the product
Trail athletes think in terms of complete systems — shoe, vest, layer, hat. Product can't be designed in isolation. The interdependencies between footwear, apparel, and gear are what make or break performance across hours of mountain running.
03
Cognition is the invisible limiter
The brain fatigues alongside the body. Decision-making quality degrades over hours of effort, and the stakes on technical terrain are real. Understanding the cognitive recovery timeline — and building strategies to extend sharpness — is a competitive frontier.
04
These athletes are one step from survival
Ultra-long durations, extreme elevation, unpredictable weather, unstable footing. Trail running pushes closer to the boundary of human survival than most sports. The gear, nutrition, and mental preparation must reflect that reality.
