Athlete Performance Investigation

From Symptom to System

An elite miler reports bilateral quadriceps heaviness, progressive power loss, and post-race dissociation during championship finals. The symptoms vanish in training. What's going on?

From the
Nike Sport Research Lab • Applied Performance Innovation
14-Day Residency
The Symptom

Something is wrong in the final 200 meters

An elite female middle-distance runner competing internationally at 1500m and the mile. During the finale of major championships — Diamond League finals, World Championship finals — the same pattern emerges: an overwhelming bilateral quadriceps heaviness, a global loss of power, then lightheadedness, narrowed awareness, and a perceived risk of syncope.

Post-race, she enters a prolonged dissociative state — diminished awareness, impaired memory recall, persistent lightheadedness lasting 15–20 minutes or more. Post-race blood lactate: >20 mmol/L.

"At its most severe, the athlete required conscious effort to remain upright and continue running."
Presenting Concern

The critical detail: these symptoms were not replicated in training. But true race-length maximal efforts had never been performed in practice — all-out efforts were limited to 200–400m. The question for the investigation: normal physiology under extreme demand, or a discrete limitation hiding somewhere in the system?

The Investigation

14 days embedded at the NSRL

The athlete's performance director reached out to the Nike Sport Research Lab. An embedded 14-day training camp was designed — the athlete arrived at Nike World Headquarters immediately following an indoor competition and trained on-site while the API team instrumented every session.

Sessions spanned the full intensity spectrum — from easy aerobic runs through maximal graded exercise testing — to build a comprehensive physiological profile. Bilateral NIRS sensors on both quadriceps tracked muscle oxygen dynamics at every intensity. The Framework

Tracing oxygen from air to muscle

The investigation was organized around the O₂ transport cascade: oxygen flows from the atmosphere through the lungs, heart, blood, and into the working muscle. At each step, there's a potential bottleneck. The team assessed every link in the chain.

The Findings

Where the ceiling lives

PRIMARY CEILING
Cardiac output at maximum
LT2 occurs at 94% of max heart rate, leaving minimal cardiac reserve above threshold. During mile repeats, HR reached 99% of maximum. The system has more extraction capacity than the heart can feed. This is the primary bottleneck at sustained race-relevant intensities.
COMPOUNDS THE CEILING
Exercise-induced arterial hypoxemia
SpO₂ dropped from 98% to 92% at exhaustion — a 6-point decline. Critically, post-visit spirometry at Newcastle confirmed supernormal lung function (FEV1 129%, FVC 118%, MVV 147 L/min) and supernormal respiratory muscle strength. The lungs are not the problem — EIAH is occurring despite a robust respiratory system, likely driven by pulmonary transit-time limitation at very high cardiac outputs. This effect would compound at altitude. EIAH remains on the table as a contributor, but the mechanism is gas exchange dynamics, not structural respiratory limitation.
ROBUST
Muscle oxidative capacity is not the bottleneck
SmO₂ holds steady at threshold. At maximal effort, extraction reaches 5–13% — deep desaturation indicating the muscles are fully capable of using every molecule of O₂ delivered. Hemoglobin mass is +33% above reference. The machinery is all there — it's just not getting all the gas it can handle.
WATCH
Right-side motor coordination deficit
Force plate assessment revealed a coordination, not strength, asymmetry on the right side that worsens under cueing demands. This was corroborated by bilateral NIRS showing asymmetric oxygen dynamics between legs. Not the primary limiter, but a contributor to efficiency loss.
"The presenting symptoms — bilateral quadriceps heaviness, progressive power loss, and post-race neurocognitive impairment — are consistent with an O₂ delivery ceiling: cardiac output at maximum compounded by EIAH, producing both local and systemic effects at race-relevant intensities. Post-visit pulmonary function testing confirmed supernormal lung capacity and respiratory muscle strength — the lungs are not the limiter. The ceiling is cardiac output of fully oxygenated blood."
Key Finding • Updated March 2026
The Path Forward

Priority interventions

With the ceiling identified, the investigation moved to actionable recommendations — targeting the specific limiters found in the cascade.

01
Stroke volume development
The highest-leverage adaptation. Aerobic volume and sustained moderate-intensity work to drive eccentric cardiac hypertrophy — more blood per beat at the same heart rate.
02
Heat & altitude acclimation
Extended heat exposure (5+ weeks) for plasma volume expansion and stroke volume gains. Altitude for Hb mass augmentation to offset EIAH. Both target the central delivery ceiling.
03
Aerobic capacity development
Raise LT1 and LT2 as a fraction of HRmax. Expand the sustainable intensity range so that race pace sits further from the cardiac ceiling.
04
Warm-up priming protocol
A 4–5 minute bout near LT2/LTP pace to prime VO₂ kinetics and elevate critical speed before racing. Addresses the gap between call-room and gun.
05
Single-leg coordinative S&C
Reintroduce complex single-leg exercises targeting the right-side coordination deficit. Not strength — coordination under load and cueing demand.
06
SpO₂ monitoring
Track EIAH severity during high-intensity sessions, racing, and at altitude. Establish a longitudinal baseline to measure whether interventions reduce the desaturation.
06b
Operating lung volumes during exercise
Assess whether critical inspiratory constraints are occurring at peak exercise — where tidal volume flattens and ventilation can only increase via breathing frequency. This would increase respiratory muscle O₂ cost and potentially worsen diaphragm fatigue. Collaboration with Newcastle.
06c
FiO₂ time trial
Perform a time trial with slightly elevated FiO₂ (above room air) to prevent EIAH and directly test whether eliminating the desaturation changes symptoms or performance. A definitive test of the EIAH contribution.
07
Address the call room situation
The dead time between warm-up and the gun is a physiological cliff. VO₂ kinetics slow, priming fades, and the athlete starts cold. Solving the call room gap is as much a competitive edge as any training block.
08
Breath-hold priming protocol
Investigate breath-hold techniques as a tool to maintain elevated VO₂ kinetics and blood flow priming through the call room window. A low-logistics intervention that can be performed in place, right up to the start line.
09
Respiratory muscle training
Despite supernormal MIPs, targeted inspiratory muscle training (e.g. PowerBreathe K series, 30 maximal breaths at 30–40% MIP, 1–2x daily) may improve respiratory muscle efficiency at high ventilations and reduce the O₂ cost of breathing — freeing more O₂ for locomotor muscles. Consultation via Newcastle collaboration.
10
Specialized performance product
A great opportunity to develop purpose-built product for exactly this moment — bridging the gap from warm-up to race. Equipment designed to preserve physiological readiness when access to movement is restricted.
