Don't just sit there at halftime.

A 3-minute priming protocol changes how professional women's soccer players start the second half.

22 professional female players. Randomized controlled trial. Sprint and jump testing before and after halftime. Three minutes of targeted movement was enough to prime the neuromuscular system — and the data showed it.

Abreu et al. (2026) — International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance

Scroll ↓
The question

What happens to performance during halftime — and can you fight it?

Halftime is a performance cliff. Players sit for 15 minutes, muscle temperature drops 1.5–2.0°C, and the neuromuscular system downshifts. When the whistle blows for the second half, athletes are asked to produce maximal efforts with a body that has already started cooling down.

This study asked a simple question: can a brief, structured re-warm-up during halftime reverse that decline — specifically in professional women's soccer?

Why it matters

This is practical, female-specific, and immediately applicable.

Designed for real halftime constraints

Only 3 minutes. No specialized equipment. FIFA 11+ exercises that teams already know. This isn't a lab protocol — it fits the actual time window coaches have to work with.

Female athlete-specific evidence

The study used professional women's soccer players exclusively — an underrepresented population in re-warm-up research. Most existing evidence comes from male cohorts. This fills a critical gap.

Post-activation performance enhancement

The protocol leverages PAPE — priming the neuromuscular system with high-intensity movements to enhance subsequent power output. The plyometric exercises and small-sided game provide exactly this stimulus.

Injury prevention angle

The second half opens with the highest injury rates in soccer. Cold muscles, reduced neural drive, and immediate high-intensity demands create a vulnerability window. Re-warm-up protocols directly address this risk.

Direct relevance to Nike team sport athletes

Any team sport with a halftime break — soccer, basketball, football — faces the same thermoregulatory and neuromuscular challenge. A validated, time-efficient solution has broad application across Nike's athlete roster.

The takeaway

Three minutes. Six exercises. One small-sided game. A measurably better second half.

Sitting through halftime is a performance decision — and it's the wrong one. A brief, structured re-warm-up combining FIFA 11+ plyometric exercises with a small-sided game meaningfully improved sprint performance and attenuated jump power decline in professional women's soccer players.

The protocol is time-efficient, evidence-based, requires no equipment, and was designed specifically for female athletes. For any team navigating the second-half performance drop, the message is clear: don't let your players cool down.
