Hemoglobin first. Hormones second.

In collaboration with the Oregon Performance Research Lab at the University of Oregon, we tested whether menstrual cycle phase changes middle-distance running performance. What actually mattered was the capacity to carry oxygen.

16 highly trained women. Two time-to-exhaustion trials timed to low and high hormone phases. Blood, muscle, and metabolic profiling at every step. Hemoglobin mass — not hormones — predicted who ran longer.

Wilkins, Richardson & Schoeberlein — Oregon Performance Research Lab, University of Oregon
Kirby — Nike Sport Research Lab

The question

Does menstrual cycle phase change how fast you run?

Estrogen and progesterone fluctuate dramatically across the menstrual cycle, affecting cardiovascular, respiratory, metabolic, and neuromuscular systems. Popular strategies claim training and racing should be timed to cycle phase.

But two major meta-analyses — McNulty et al. (2020) and Schlie et al. (2025) — tell the same story. The 2020 review found only a "trivial detrimental effect" of the early follicular phase, and half the studies didn't even verify hormone levels with bloodwork. The 2025 review, which only included studies that verified hormones via blood testing, confirmed that aerobic performance is largely unaffected — though submaximal performance and peak power may fluctuate.

Our approach: verify hormones, test performance, and measure everything in between — in athletes running to exhaustion in about 4 minutes. Middle-distance events are supramaximal (~105–115% VO₂max), demanding both a high aerobic ceiling and rapid oxygen delivery. This study asks what actually predicts performance across hormone profiles.

The protocol

Four visits. Every system measured.

Visit 2 — Familiarization

Visits 3 & 4 — The Key Trials

Blood draw E₂, P₄, testosterone, LH, FSH, SHBG, iron panel
TTE trial All-out run to exhaustion (~16.7 kph / 5:47 min/mile)
Continuous VO₂ kinetics, %SmO₂ (Moxy NIRS), HR, lactate
CO rebreathe Hb mass, blood volume, RBCV, PV (Detalo system)
Timing 13–15 days apart (low vs high hormone)

Hormone status was verified by serum blood analysis (mass spectrometry via QUEST Diagnostics), not assumed from calendar. Muscle oxygenation was tracked bilaterally on the rectus femoris at 1 Hz. All hormone, hematological, and performance data were analyzed using linear mixed models — the most rigorous statistical approach for repeated-measures designs.

Of the 15 women who completed the study, three did not demonstrate clear P₄ fluctuations between visits — their trials could not be classified into low and high hormone days. Of the remaining 12, seven ran shorter on the high hormone day, two were similar, and three ran longer.

A linear mixed model controlling for E₂, LH, FSH, and bioavailable testosterone confirmed that progesterone had no significant effect on TTE duration (P = 0.072). No sex hormone predicted performance.

So if the hormones themselves don't explain the variation — what does?

The predictors

Oxygen capacity predicts performance. Hormones don't.

When we looked at which physiological characteristics actually predicted how fast these athletes ran, the results were overwhelming. LTP, VO₂max, and critical speed are all highly correlated to each other (r = 0.83–0.92, P < 0.001) — and to muscle oxidative capacity:

The message is clear: your physiology — not your cycle phase — determines how fast you run. NIRS-derived muscle oxidative capacity — a non-invasive measure of mitochondrial function — is now validated against VO₂max, LTP, and critical speed across 16 athletes. Hormones predicted none of it.

The real finding

It's oxygen-carrying capacity — not hormones.

We compared hematological variables between each participant's longest and shortest TTE days — independent of hormone profile. The day they ran longer, they had significantly more hemoglobin and less dilution:

More red cells = longer runs

Higher RBCV was positively associated with TTE duration. More oxygen carriers in the blood means more oxygen delivered to working muscle at supramaximal intensities.

More plasma = shorter runs

Higher PV dilutes the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. The effect was nearly symmetric — every 100 mL of added plasma cost ~16 seconds of performance.

Hormones don't drive the hematological variation

The linear mixed model found no significant relationship between sex hormone concentrations and Hb mass, RBCV, or PV. The variation in these variables occurs independently of hormone fluctuations.

The twist

When hemoglobin is low, fixing it overwhelms everything else.

Three of the 12 participants actually improved on the high hormone day. We explored what made them different — and found a striking pattern:

9.49 g/kg

Hb mass of the 3 improvers on the low hormone day — 1.66 g/kg below the group mean (11.15 ± 1.09) and outside one standard deviation

These three athletes had the lowest relative hemoglobin mass in the study. On their high hormone visit, their Hb mass and RBCV increased — and their performance improved. Their oxygen-carrying capacity went up, and that effect was large enough to override any hormonal influence.

Remove the 3 improvers and the picture flips

When these three participants were excluded from the analysis, a significant difference in TTE emerged between low and high hormone days (P = 0.017). The change in Hb mass and RBCV strongly influenced the overall group outcome.

Hemoglobin status modulates the hormone response

When Hb mass and RBCV are relatively low, improvements in these variables exert a stronger influence on performance than hormonal variation alone. But when hematological variables are adequate, hormone profile may still underlie performance outcomes.

This is the nuance the group average hides: the hormone effect exists — but only when hemoglobin is sufficient. When it's not, the oxygen-carrying capacity signal is so powerful it overwhelms everything else.

What this means

Stop timing the cycle. Start tracking oxygen capacity.

For coaches & athletes

Menstrual cycle phase alone doesn't predict performance in trained women. The popular advice to periodize training around cycle phase isn't supported by this data. What matters is the capacity to carry and consume oxygen — hemoglobin mass, red blood cell volume, and muscle oxidative capacity.

For sport science

The individual variability is the finding. Group averages mask the modulating role of hematological status. Future work should track Hb mass and RBCV alongside hormone profiles — because when oxygen-carrying capacity is low, its recovery overwhelms any hormonal effect.

For Breaking4

If the 4-minute mile hinges on oxygen delivery — and the Exeter model says it does — then hemoglobin mass is the variable that matters most. Protecting and optimizing the capacity to carry oxygen is the priority. NIRS-derived muscle oxidative capacity is validated as a non-invasive window into the mitochondrial side of the equation.

The takeaway

The cycle doesn't predict performance. Hemoglobin mass does.

Across 16 highly trained women and two supramaximal trials each, we found no group effect of hormone profile on running performance. But the individual variation told a deeper story: red blood cell volume and plasma volume are the levers. Every 100 mL of additional RBCV buys 11 seconds. Every 100 mL of added plasma costs 16. And when hemoglobin mass is low, its recovery overwhelms any hormonal signal — the three athletes who improved on the high hormone day were the three with the lowest Hb mass. Muscle oxidative capacity, validated via NIRS against VO₂max, LTP, and critical speed, confirms that the oxygen equation runs from the blood to the mitochondria. Hormones are not the rate limiter.
