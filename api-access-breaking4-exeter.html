The study

What do you test to predict a 4-minute mile?

Middle-distance running is a collision of aerobic and anaerobic physiology. The 1500m sits right at the crossover — too long to sprint, too short to rely on endurance alone.

Exeter's team brought 21 elite middle-distance runners (11 male, 10 female) through a comprehensive battery of lab and field tests to identify which physiological variables actually predict 4-minute performance.

The models

Two equations. One answer.

4-Minute Time Trial Model
4MTT = 247.8(CS) + 4.1(D′/CS) − 26.7
87%
of variance in 4-minute performance explained

Critical Speed (CS) — the aerobic engine

The strongest single predictor (r = 0.84). CS represents the highest speed you can sustain in a metabolic steady state — your aerobic ceiling.

D′/CS — the anaerobic ratio

D′ is your finite anaerobic reserve above critical speed. The ratio D′/CS captures how much kick you have relative to your aerobic base. Both variables were significant (P < 0.001).

Exeter's 1500m prediction model using 20s sprint speed and lactate turnpoint. The model explains 81% of variance with near-perfect prediction accuracy (P = 0.98).

1500m Prediction Model
1500m(s) = −18.5(Sprint) − 6.1(LTP) + 494
81%
of variance in 1500m time explained

Both models tell the same story: you need both engines. The aerobic system sets the floor. The anaerobic system determines how far above it you can go.

Inside the lab

Testing the world record holder

With the models built, the team brought the world's fastest 1500m runner into the Exeter lab for a full physiological assessment. Here's what they found.

The gap

Where the 4-minute barrier lives

Aerobic engine: extraordinary

Running economy among the best ever measured. Lactate turnpoint at 19 km/h. She is more than strong enough on the aerobic side — "she could afford to lose a little 5000m performance capability as long as it were balanced by improved 800m capability."

Anaerobic capacity: the limiter

Low peak lactate values, limited D' (149m), and a modest VLaMax (0.39 mM/s) all point to the same conclusion: her anaerobic system doesn't match her aerobic engine. This is where the 4-minute gap lives.

VO₂max: room to push

At 65.2 mL/kg/min, there's headroom to push closer to 70. Targeted interval sessions at 3000m pace — not more mileage — are the prescription.

The Exeter analysis concludes: her profile resembles an elite distance runner, not a middle-distance specialist. The 4-minute mile demands a shift — less endurance bias, more anaerobic power — without losing what makes her extraordinary.

The roadmap

What needs to change

70

Target VO₂max (mL/kg/min). Currently 65.2 — push it closer with 3000m-pace intervals: 4-5 x 1200m, 6-8 x 800m, or 12-16 x 400m with short recovery.

↑ D′

Build anaerobic capacity through speed endurance work: 8-12 x 200m, 6-8 x 300m, or 4-6 x 400m at 400-800m race pace with full recovery.

↑ V̇Lamax

Increase the rate of anaerobic energy production. Recruit more fast-twitch fibers. Improve the ability to generate — and tolerate — lactate.

The key insight: less volume, higher intensity, longer recovery. The training shift isn't about running more — it's about running differently.

The takeaway

The aerobic engine is built. The barrier is anaerobic.

Exeter's models show that 4-minute performance demands both high critical speed and a substantial anaerobic reserve. When they tested the world record holder, the data was unambiguous: extraordinary aerobic efficiency, but an anaerobic system that doesn't yet match. The formula for sub-4 isn't about getting fitter — it's about getting faster.
